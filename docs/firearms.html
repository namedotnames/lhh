The Unspoken Cost of Firearm Access

Most American firearm deaths are suicides

Lily Hoagland

Published October 14, 2019

In the wake of every mass shooting in America comes the debate over gun control. Amidst loud choruses for or againt reform, one vital statistic hums softly unheard: the majority of gun deaths are not homicides. They're suicides.

The largest percentage jump for both homicides and suicides with a firearm occurred between the years 2014-2016.

Having access to a firearm when suicidal greatly impacts whether the attempt's outcome will be fatal or not. Over 80 percent of suicide attempts involving a gun ended up in death, significantly above other methods, according to a 2001 poll by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

Veterans Are Especially At Risk

Due to a propensity towards mental health issues and familiarity with guns, veterans tend to overwhelmingly choose a firearm to commit suicide.

Social Stigma

Only 23 percent of Americans said suicide was the leading cause of gun death, according to a July poll by APM Research Labs.

Understanding the lack of public awareness requires looking beyond the polarizing gun rights debate to the social stigma attached to taking one's own life. "Culturally and historically, suicide has always come with a high level of shame and embarrassment," according to Lisa Gold, Georgetown University Professor of Psychiatry.
